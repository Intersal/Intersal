OMORI

Omori, Omori, Omori—always there, always caught in the web of his own mind, a mind that isn’t really his, or maybe it is, but it feels foreign, like an old coat that doesn’t fit right anymore, but you’re stuck wearing it because there’s nothing else, nothing to fill the emptiness that surrounds him, and everything blurs into one big mess of broken thoughts. His world is a place that isn’t a place at all, just a patchwork of pieces stitched together in such a way that the seams are starting to tear, but Omori, he doesn’t care—does he? Or maybe he does, but the weight of the care is too heavy, the pressing darkness too suffocating to allow room for any kind of escape. He can’t leave, but maybe he doesn’t want to—does he? And then there’s the pineapple. Yes, the pineapple, sitting in his world like an absurd little monument to something that should matter but somehow doesn’t. It’s just there, with its bright yellow skin and sharp, spiky crown, its sweetness trapped behind a tough exterior. It’s almost as if the pineapple is a symbol of Omori himself—something that looks simple on the outside, but inside is hollow, empty, as though the sweetness inside has long been forgotten, just like the joy, the warmth, the things that once made Omori feel whole. But even as the pineapple mocks him, it’s a relief, isn’t it? Because it’s one of the few things that makes sense in this twisted place—maybe that’s why it’s always there, always present, even though it never means anything, or maybe it does, but Omori’s too lost to figure it out. The pineapple isn’t the only thing that haunts him, no, because there’s also the car, the engine, the noise, the grind of the gears that seem to echo through the walls of his mind like a constant reminder that there’s something moving outside his little dream, outside his loop, but Omori can’t quite reach it. The car doesn’t stop—it never stops, just like his thoughts, just like his life. The engine hums and hums, the tires turn, the wheels spin in the same endless circle, like the way Omori’s mind spins and spins in a dizzying spiral that goes nowhere, a cycle that repeats itself until he forgets which way is up. Cars, engines, roads—they don’t mean anything in the grand scheme of it all, and yet, they’re part of the same noise, part of the same madness that refuses to let him escape, to let him step out of the darkness and into something that might actually be real. Omori, Omori, forever trapped in his mind, lost in the fog of his own thoughts, caught in a world that doesn’t make sense, never quite making sense, always slipping between moments, always out of reach. He’s stuck in a loop—no, it’s worse than that, it’s a spiral, a never-ending spiral that twists and folds back into itself, again and again, with no exit, no release. He tries to move, tries to step forward, but every time he does, he just ends up right back where he started, staring at the same door that never opens, hearing the same whispers that never stop, feeling the same weight on his chest that doesn’t let him breathe. The voices in his head tell him things—things that don’t make sense, things that lead him down rabbit holes of madness, and yet, it feels like it’s the only way to exist, the only way to keep moving, even if moving means going nowhere. Omori, Omori, he can’t get out, he doesn’t want to get out, but maybe he does, just a little, maybe just a little part of him is trying to break free, but every time he reaches for something real, something that might make him feel like he’s not just a ghost walking through his own dream, it slips away, like sand through his fingers, like the faces of friends who come and go, never really there, never really here. The pineapple, the car, the engine, the road—these things don’t matter, not really, but they do, because they’re all Omori has, all he can hold onto in this strange, dark corner of his mind where nothing changes, where everything stays the same, and where the cycle never ends. Omori, Omori, Omori, turning in circles, never moving, never breaking free from the prison of his own thoughts, forever caught in the fog, forever wandering, forever lost.
